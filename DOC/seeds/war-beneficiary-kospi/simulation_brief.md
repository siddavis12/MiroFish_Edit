# Simulation Brief: 전쟁 수혜주 코스피 투자 (단기/중기/장기)

## Simulation Requirement

Simulate the social media discourse around war-beneficiary stocks (전쟁 수혜주) on the Korean KOSPI market, examining short-term, mid-term, and long-term investment perspectives.

**IMPORTANT: All simulation output texts — including agent posts, comments, replies, and report content — MUST be written in Korean (한국어). The agents represent Korean market participants communicating on Korean social media platforms.**

[Topic & Context]
Geopolitical tensions — including the prolonged Russia-Ukraine war, Middle East conflicts (Israel-Iran, Hormuz Strait blockade risks), Taiwan Strait escalation, and Korean Peninsula instability — have driven a surge in defense, energy, shipbuilding, and cybersecurity stocks on the KOSPI. As of early 2026, defense stocks such as Hanwha Aerospace, LIG Nex1, Hyundai Rotem, and Korea Aerospace Industries (KAI) have posted 16–44% gains, while energy names like S-Oil and SK Innovation rally on oil price spikes. HD Korea Shipbuilding and HMM benefit from rising freight rates, and cybersecurity firms Ahnlab and Igloo Corporation see demand for critical infrastructure protection.

[Simulation Objectives]
Key questions:
1. Which war-beneficiary sectors and stocks do market participants favor at each time horizon — short-term (1–3 months), mid-term (6–12 months), and long-term (2–5 years)?
2. How does the "take-profit after short-term surge vs. hold for long-term growth" debate evolve across retail investors, institutional investors, and analysts?
3. Do consensus picks emerge, or does the discourse fragment into competing sector-rotation narratives?

Observation focus: The tension between momentum-driven short-term trading and conviction-based long-term accumulation, especially around defense stocks.

[Expected Actor Types]
- Defense companies: Hanwha Aerospace (K9, Chunmoo), LIG Nex1 (missiles), Hyundai Rotem (tanks), KAI (aircraft), Hanwha Systems (radar/C4I), SNT Dynamics, Poongsan (ammunition)
- Energy/refining companies: S-Oil, SK Innovation, GS Energy, Heungkuk Oil
- Shipbuilding/shipping firms: HD Korea Shipbuilding, HD Hyundai Heavy Industries, Hanwha Ocean, HMM
- Cybersecurity firms: Ahnlab, Igloo Corporation
- Securities analysts: Korea Investment Securities (analyst Jang Nam-hyun), Samsung Securities, NH Investment, Yuanta Securities — issuing buy/hold/sell calls and target prices
- Institutional investors: National Pension Service (NPS), foreign institutions (BlackRock, etc.) — large block trades visible in disclosure data
- Government agencies: Defense Acquisition Program Administration (DAPA), Ministry of National Defense, Ministry of Trade — policy decisions affecting order books
- Financial media: Hankyung, Maeil Business, stock-focused YouTubers and bloggers — amplifying narratives and shaping retail sentiment
- Retail investors: Individual traders on online communities (Naver Stock, DCinside Stock Gallery) debating entry/exit timing

[Interaction Dynamics]
- Analysts publish bullish reports → retail investors pile in → short-term price surge → profit-taking debate erupts
- Institutional investors accumulate quietly → disclosure triggers media coverage → second wave of retail FOMO
- Government announces defense budget increase or arms export deal → defense stocks gap up → "already priced in" vs. "just the beginning" argument
- Geopolitical escalation event → broad war-theme rally → sector rotation debate (defense vs. energy vs. shipbuilding)
- Long-term bulls cite structural rearmament trend (NATO spending, K-defense exports) vs. short-term bears warning of valuation stretch

[Scenarios]
1. Consensus convergence: Market settles on 2–3 "must-own" names (e.g., Hanwha Aerospace for long-term, energy names for short-term hedge), reducing volatility
2. Polarized debate: Sharp divide between "sell the rally" camp and "defense supercycle" camp, with high volatility and frequent sentiment reversals
3. Sector rotation narrative: Attention shifts sequentially — defense → energy → shipbuilding → cybersecurity — as each sector's catalyst fades and the next emerges

[Language Requirement]
All agent-generated content in this simulation MUST be produced in Korean (한국어). This includes social media posts, comments, replies, discussions, and any textual output. The simulation represents Korean market participants on Korean-language platforms such as Naver, DCinside, and Korean financial forums.
